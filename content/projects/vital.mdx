## what it is

A personal health database connected to the same Vault that powers VANTAGE. Vital handles the four logging surfaces every fitness tracker handles — meals, gym sessions, daily activity, weight — and adds a Claude layer on top that turns the data into narrative reviews, trend analysis, and next-workout suggestions.

The data lives in Supabase. A sync job mirrors the structured logs into the Vault, so Claude has health context in any other conversation — not just inside the Vital app.

## the four logging surfaces

- **meal log** — point the camera at the plate, Claude vision identifies ingredients, estimates macros. Low-confidence items surface as one-tap checkboxes the user can correct.
- **workout log** — voice input is the primary interface. &quot;four sets of squats, 80kg, 8 reps, last set was rough&quot; → Claude Sonnet parses to structured exercise + load + reps + RPE.
- **activity** — passive: steps, heart-rate zones, active minutes pulled from the OS health APIs. No manual entry required.
- **weight + body comp** — manual once-a-week, with a single Claude-generated note prompting the user to log mood/sleep alongside.

## the AI layer

Three jobs:

**weekly review.** Every Sunday, Claude pulls the trailing seven days of logs and writes a one-paragraph review: surplus or deficit, training volume vs the prior week, what changed, what to keep an eye on. Same citation discipline as the BPS Generator — every claim references a specific log entry.

**next-workout suggester.** Reads the user&apos;s training history (sessions, exercises, loads, RPE), models fatigue and recovery, suggests a session for today with rationale. The user can accept, modify, or skip; the response feeds back into the model&apos;s next suggestion.

**vault digest.** A nightly job pushes a structured summary to the Vault. So when I&apos;m chatting with VANTAGE about something completely unrelated and the conversation touches on energy or focus, the bot already knows I undertrained this week and slept poorly twice. The context flows without the user re-typing it.

## why this and not another macro tracker

Three reasons existing apps are wrong:

1. they make you type or scan barcodes; that friction is why people quit by week three
2. they don&apos;t connect to anything else in your life — your phone tracker is a silo
3. they don&apos;t use the data for anything beyond a daily total — no narrative review, no compounding context

Vital fixes all three. Claude vision kills the typing on meals, voice kills the typing on workouts. Vault sync kills the silo. The weekly review and workout suggester turn the data into something that compounds.

## what i learned

The right way to evaluate a vision-driven product isn&apos;t accuracy on a benchmark. It&apos;s the friction-to-log curve. If logging takes 4 seconds, people log; if it takes 40, they don&apos;t. The model&apos;s job is to win the friction war, even at the cost of some accuracy that the user can correct in the next two seconds anyway.

The sync-to-Vault pattern generalizes. Anything I track on a phone — health, finances, sleep, reading — is more useful when the bot can read it without me copying it. The phone app is the capture surface; the Vault is the long-term memory; Claude is the layer that connects them.
